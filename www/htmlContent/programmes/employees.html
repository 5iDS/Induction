In this induction programme, we will focus on the "4 steps to safety", an informal assessment that each employee should undertake regardless of his position at Phola before commencing any task.

These assessments are designed to be simple tools. These can be used every day by all employees and contractors to assess risks, primarily at the operational task level.

The Foor Steps to Zero Harm to be followed are:


  • Stop - and think through the task you or your team are about to do. Ask yourself - what are we going to be doing today?
    Discuss the task for one minute to ensure the team understands exactly what they are going to be doing.
  • Look - Around and identify potential hazards. Ask yourself - Do you have the correct tools or equipment and are they safe?
  • Assess - the level of risk for the hazard/s identified. Ask yourself - Will something go wrong today if I use the incorrect tools or equipment and what could happen to me or others?
  • Manage - the risk according to the Risk Level. Ask yourself - What are you going to do about it now to prevent an injury to myself and others?

In the interest of Health, Safety and the Environment at Phola, our supervisors also conduct Planned Task Workplace Observations.

The steps to conducting these observations are:


  • Observe, then get the persons attention.
  • Comment on what the employee was doing safely.
  • Discuss with the employee:
    • The possible consequences of the unsafe act.
    • Safer ways to do the job.
    • Be open and direct, but not confrontational.
  • Get the employees agreement to work safely in the future.
  • Discuss other safety issues of the the job.
    • Encourage the employee to discuss safety concerns and offer ideas.
  • Thank the employee.

Hazard or Zero Energy Significant Event Reporting, is a critically important part of our safety, health and environment strategy at Phola.

Events can be classified into different categories:


  • Potential incidents. for example, uneven steps.
  • Zero Energy. for example, a damaged ladder.
  • Near miss. for example, Someone walking under a suspended load.
  • Injury or property damage. for example, a slip and fall where there is either property damage or personal injury.

All incidents must be reported to your supervisor or the Health and Safety Representative, either verbally or using the HSEC event reporting book.

It is better to report any potential problems before someone gets hurt or seriously injured. This must be done before the end of the working shift on the working day of the incident.

The identification and immediate management of potential hazards is a critical factor in maintaining a safe work environment.

By identifying and reporting incidents at your workplace to our supervisors, you will be showing leadership and reducing the potential for incidents which could impact adversely on our operation. For example; this could be a place where someone could slip and fall. Perhaps nothing happened, but could if not reported.
